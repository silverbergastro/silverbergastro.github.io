Finding new disks with citizen science

I am the principal investigator for Disk Detective version 2.0, a citizen science Website formed as part of a partnership between NASA GSFC and the Zooniverse project to identify new circumstellar disks in the WISE catalog. On the Disk Detective site, citizen scientists look through a "flipbook" of images of the same target, from optical to mid-infrared, before sorting the subject into given qualitative categories. The "good candidates" get passed on to the science team, which organizes a literature review of each potential target, and then submits it for follow-up observation as a Disk Detective Object of Interest (DDOI). DDv2.0 improves on the original incarnation by incorporating data from Pan-STARRS PS1, Gaia, Skymapper, and the unWISE coadds of WISE data to provide improved spatial resolution to identify targets. It also slightly lowers the W4 signal-to-noise threshold, enabling a search for closer-by disks with moderate (SNR >5) signal at W4, and incorporates the Zooniverse Project Builder framework, which allows for more adaptation and refocusing of work as necessary. Our goal here is to identify additional Peter Pan Disks, to better constrain how common these systems are. You can read about the launch (and get caught up on the ongoing activities of the project) on our blog.

Disk Detective 1.0

I performed the research for my doctoral dissertation as the lead graduate researcher on the science team for Disk Detective version 1.0, We go over our classification and follow-up process, and present our first set of new disk candidates, in Kuchner, Silverberg, et al. 2016. In Silverberg et al. 2018, we used website classification data from DDv1.0 and high-resolution follow-up imaging to show that only ~21,600 systems with infrared excess in AllWISE (with W4 SNR>10) will actually be good disk candidates. We also presented estimated false positive disk identification rates for other published disk searches, used the Disk Detective methodology to evaluate the Theissen & West (2014) disk survey, and present 244 new disk candidates, including 31 within 125 pc of the Sun.
